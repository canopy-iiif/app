  <h1>Tracing light across a single collection</h1>
  <p>
    Every Canopy project starts with a point of view. This template uses the
    homepage as a long-form essay so visitors immediately land in your
    narrative—paragraphs, inline IIIF media, and cited works replace the usual
    grid of cards. Keep writing until the story feels complete; the navigation
    stays minimal so readers remain in this flow.
  </p>
  <p>
    The structure below mirrors how authors sketch catalog essays: an opening
    thesis, field notes, a close look at one manifest, and references. Use it as
    scaffolding when composing your own entry point.

<Container>
  <h2>Set your thesis with a few focused paragraphs</h2>
  <p>
    Lead with the why. These opening sections explain what the reader should pay
    attention to before they ever scroll past the fold. In practice that could
    mean summarizing a curatorial question, explaining how the manifests were
    sourced, or setting up the lens you plan to use throughout the essay.
  </p>
  <p>
    Long-form markdown keeps the authoring experience approachable. Use plain
    headings, ordered lists, blockquotes, or callouts—anything you can write in
    a text editor works here. When you need a different rhythm, drop a component
    such as `<Image />`, `<Slider />`, or `<Viewer />` directly into the prose.
  </p>
  <blockquote>
    This essay template intentionally avoids grids or teaser cards. Treat it as
    a publication entry: one primary column, thoughtful typography, and a few
    inline media moments to break up the copy.
  </blockquote>
</Container>

<Container>
  <h2>Bring a manifest into focus</h2>

    inherit backlinks through `<References />`, so readers can move from a work
    to any essay that cites it.

<Container>
  <h2>Explain how the project is assembled</h2>
  <p>
    After the primary story beat, document how the collection is structured.
    This section doubles as guidance for collaborators: you can note how the IIIF
    cache is configured, which manifests power the featured hero, or how the
    search facets are tagged.
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>Drop in manifest IDs under `canopy.yml → featured` to rotate the hero.</li>
    <li>
      Keep the `referencedManifests` array in this file updated so citations stay
      in sync.
    </li>
    <li>
      Use `content/notes/index.mdx` for production notes, bibliographies, or
      instructions for future editors.
    </li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Because this template is text-first, feel free to stack multiple sections
    like this—each `<Container>` becomes another chapter.
  </p>
</Container>

<Container>
  <h2>Next steps</h2>
  <p>
    When you are ready to expand, add directories under `content/` for more
    essays or supporting context. Work pages will continue to generate from the
    IIIF sources you configured in `canopy.yml`, so the essay acts as the
    narrative cover for the deeper catalog. We recommend tackling the following
    checklist after cloning the template:
  </p>
  <ol>
    <li>Swap in your own manifests and regenerate the IIIF cache.</li>
    <li>Replace the hero copy and heading structure in this file.</li>
    <li>Update `content/navigation.yml` to match your preferred nav labels.</li>
    <li>Add a bibliography or acknowledgements to `content/notes/index.mdx`.</li>
  </ol>
</Container>
